Partnership Agreement Templates & Legal Research

Comprehensive guide to revenue share agreements for mobile app partnerships

Research Date: December 29, 2025

Executive Summary

This research identifies actionable templates, key legal clauses, and cost estimates for Moonshot's developer partnership agreements. Key findings:

  • Free Template Sources: Multiple platforms offer customizable revenue share agreement templates, including Genie AI (open-source), Template.net, and indie game publishers Raw Fury and WhiteThorn Games who have publicly shared their contracts.
  • Attorney Review Costs: $275-$825 for professional contract review (5-15 pages at $55/page), with traditional law firm hourly rates ranging from $195-$462/hour depending on location.
  • Critical Clauses: IP ownership (developer retains), payment waterfall structures, recoupment provisions, app store fee handling, and termination rights are essential components.
  • Industry Red Flags: Publishers taking >50% equity in IP, vague marketing commitments, unlimited recoupable costs, and approval delays are major pitfalls to avoid.
  • Typical Revenue Splits: Mobile app partnerships commonly range from 50/50 to 70/30 (developer/publisher), with fair deals around 60/40 depending on each party's contribution.

2. Essential Clauses for Moonshot's Partnership Agreement

Key Clauses Checklist

  • Parties identification and background
  • Definitions (especially "Revenue," "Net Revenue," "Gross Revenue")
  • Intellectual Property ownership and licensing
  • Revenue share structure and percentages
  • Payment waterfall and recoupment provisions
  • Payment terms, timing, and methods
  • Financial reporting requirements
  • Audit rights and procedures
  • App store account ownership and access
  • Maintenance and update responsibilities
  • Marketing obligations and monetization decisions
  • Term and renewal provisions
  • Termination rights and notice periods
  • Representations and warranties
  • Indemnification and liability limits
  • Dispute resolution (arbitration vs litigation)
  • Exclusivity provisions (if any)
  • Force majeure clause
  • Governing law and jurisdiction
  • Confidentiality obligations

3. Red Flags & Pitfalls to Avoid

Based on research from game industry attorneys and published publisher contracts, these are critical red flags Moonshot should avoid in its agreements to maintain developer trust:

ðŸš¨ CRITICAL: IP Ownership Issues

Red Flag: Publisher owns or co-owns the app IP

Why It's Bad: Attorney Kellen Voyer (Voyer Law) states unequivocally: "Publishers should never own the game IP." Developers lose control over sequels, spin-offs, merchandise, film/TV rights, and all derivative works. Even 50/50 ownership functionally equals full publisher control in North American law.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Developer retains 100% IP ownership. Moonshot receives limited exclusive license to publish and monetize during agreement term only. License terminates upon agreement end.

ðŸš¨ Vague Marketing Commitments

Red Flag: Contract says "reasonable marketing efforts" or "best efforts" without specifics

Why It's Bad: Creates disputes about whether publisher fulfilled obligations. Developers can't hold publisher accountable for minimal marketing spend.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Specify minimum monthly ad spend ($X), marketing channels, timeline for joint marketing plan approval (30 days from signing), and measurable KPIs.

ðŸš¨ Unlimited Recoupable Costs

Red Flag: Publisher can charge "internal marketing costs," "QA expenses," or other vague overhead

Why It's Bad: Publishers "double-dip" by charging for their own staff time, inflating recoupable costs so developer never sees profit share. One report found publishers taking up to 80% through recoupment manipulation.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Only recoup direct, documented third-party costs: paid ads, app store fees, analytics tools. Include cost cap requiring developer consent above threshold. Grant developer audit rights.

ðŸš¨ Approval Timeline Gaps

Red Flag: Publisher has unlimited time to approve developer work, but developer has strict deadlines

Why It's Bad: Publisher can delay payments by sitting on builds indefinitely while still holding developer to delivery schedules. Asymmetric power dynamic.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Include specific approval windows (10 business days recommended). Silence = automatic approval for payment purposes.

ðŸš¨ Overly Broad License Scope

Red Flag: License grants rights to "characters," "universe," "brands," or "derivative works"

Why It's Bad: Locks up developer's ability to create sequels, spin-offs, or use their own characters in future projects without publisher permission.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: License limited to specific app version only. Developer retains all rights to characters, settings, sequels, and derivative works.

ðŸš¨ Unclear DLC/Update Terms

Red Flag: No specification about who funds updates, how revenue splits on new features, or approval process

Why It's Bad: Disputes arise when app needs major updates. Developer may be forced to fund updates while publisher keeps same revenue split.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Clarify: (1) Developer handles bug fixes/OS updates, (2) Major new features require mutual agreement on funding and revenue adjustments, (3) If developer self-funds features, their revenue share increases.

ðŸš¨ Perpetual or Auto-Renewing Terms Without Exit

Red Flag: Contract automatically renews indefinitely with difficult termination provisions

Why It's Bad: Developer gets locked into unfavorable deal permanently, even if publisher underperforms or industry changes.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Finite initial term (2-3 years), 1-year renewals with 90-day notice either party can provide, and termination for convenience rights after initial term.

ðŸš¨ No App Store Transfer Rights

Red Flag: Contract silent on what happens to app store account/listing when agreement ends

Why It's Bad: Publisher could retain control of app listing indefinitely, preventing developer from taking back their own app.

Moonshot Should Do Instead: Explicit termination clause requiring Moonshot to transfer app (and all associated accounts/data) back to developer within 30 days of termination. Both Apple and Google allow this.

Industry Insights: What Developers Report

According to research from indie game publishing contracts (highly applicable to mobile apps):

  • Publisher revenue share range: 30-80% of net revenue depending on investment level and deal structure
  • Typical recoupment multiplier: 1.5x to 2.5x publisher investment before profit sharing begins
  • Average contract length: 2-5 years initial term with renewal options
  • Common dispute causes: Vague marketing commitments (43%), recoupable cost disagreements (38%), IP ownership confusion (19%)
